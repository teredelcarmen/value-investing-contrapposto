Section I

The foundation of value investing.

Everything starts here. The core insight that has driven the world's greatest investors for nearly 100 years.

"A financial balance sheet value investor is an investor who makes their investment judgments based upon the value of assets in place rather than growth potential. Growth is icing on top."
Core Principle 1
Price ≠ Value
Mr. Market is an emotional business partner who offers you prices every day. Sometimes he's euphoric and prices too high. Sometimes he's depressed and prices too low. Your job is to exploit his irrationality — never be ruled by it.
Core Principle 2
Margin of safety
Buy assets at a significant discount to their intrinsic value. This gap protects you from errors in analysis, unforeseen events, and bad luck. The bigger the discount, the safer the investment. Graham called it the three most important words in investing.
Core Principle 3
Circle of competence
Only invest in businesses you truly understand. Knowing what you don't know is as important as what you do know. Buffett passed on tech for decades — not because he was wrong about tech, but because he knew he couldn't value it with confidence.
The two schools within value investing
Graham school — asset-based
Statistical cheapness
Buy a diversified basket of statistically cheap stocks — low P/B, low P/E, high dividend yield. Don't need to understand each business deeply. Works best in inefficient markets. Graham's "net-nets" and screen-based approach.

Key thinkers: Ben Graham, Walter Schloss, Seth Klarman (early)
Key texts: Security Analysis, The Intelligent Investor
Munger/Buffett school — quality at price
Wonderful companies at fair prices
Pay up for quality — businesses with durable competitive advantages, high ROIC, and excellent management. Hold forever. "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." — Buffett

Key thinkers: Buffett, Munger, Tom Russo, Chuck Akre
Key texts: Essays of Warren Buffett, Poor Charlie's Almanack

Section II

Ben Graham's 10 screens.

The original quantitative value investing framework. A stock that passes all 10 screens is statistically very cheap — Graham called these "defensive" investments. Even passing 7–8 indicates deep value.

How to use these: Run these as filters on a stock screener (Capital IQ, Bloomberg, Finviz). Companies passing 6+ screens historically outperform significantly. They won't be exciting — that's the point.
1
PE below inverse of AAA bond yield
If AAA bond yield = 5%, then max P/E = 20x (1/0.05). Ensures equity earnings yield exceeds bond yield — compensates for equity risk.
P/E < (1 / AAA Bond Yield) — e.g., if yield = 5%, P/E < 20x
2
PE less than 40% of 5-year average PE
Current P/E must be deeply below the stock's own historical average. Forces you to buy when the market has beaten a stock down — not when it's at highs.
Current P/E < 0.4 × (5-year average P/E)
3
Dividend yield > two-thirds of AAA bond yield
The dividend alone should provide meaningful return relative to the risk-free alternative. Ensures income while you wait for value to be recognized.
Div Yield > (2/3) × AAA Corporate Bond Yield
4
Price less than two-thirds of book value
You're buying assets at a discount to what accountants say they're worth. This is the purest form of "buying a dollar for sixty-six cents." Classic net asset value investing.
P/B < 0.67 — Price < (2/3) × Book Value per Share
5
Price less than two-thirds of net current assets
The famous "net-net" screen. You're paying less than 66 cents for every dollar of current assets (cash, receivables, inventory) after subtracting ALL liabilities. Extremely rare today — more common in small/micro caps.
Price < (2/3) × (Current Assets − Total Liabilities)
6
Debt-to-equity ratio below one (book value)
Conservative balance sheet. Total debt should not exceed total equity. Protects against distress if business deteriorates. Graham wanted financial fortress, not leverage.
Total Debt / Book Value of Equity < 1.0
7
Current assets greater than twice current liabilities
Current ratio above 2.0x. The company can pay off all short-term obligations twice over with short-term assets. Strong liquidity buffer — won't face near-term cash crunch.
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities > 2.0x
8
Total debt less than twice net current assets
Even total long-term debt should be manageable relative to liquid assets. Combines leverage and liquidity — ensures the company isn't technically solvent but practically trapped.
Total Debt < 2 × (Current Assets − Current Liabilities)
9
Historical EPS growth greater than 7% over 10 years
Not just cheap — the earnings power should be growing. Compounding at 7% doubles in ~10 years. Graham wanted to avoid value traps — businesses that are cheap because they're genuinely declining.
10-year EPS CAGR > 7%
10
No more than two years of negative earnings in 10 years
Business quality filter. A company that's been profitable 8 of 10 years is fundamentally sound. Chronic losers fail this — they're cheap because they destroy capital, not because they're misunderstood.
Max 2 years with EPS < 0 over trailing 10-year period
Graham's net-net formula:
NCAV (Net Current Asset Value) = Current Assets − Total Liabilities
Buy when: Market Cap < 0.67 × NCAV — the ultimate deep value screen.

The full toolkit — valuation methodologies
1. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) ↓

The theoretically correct method — value = present value of all future free cash flows. Best for stable, predictable businesses with long operating histories. Most sensitive to terminal growth rate and discount rate assumptions.

DCF formula
Intrinsic Value = Σ [FCFₜ / (1+WACC)ᵗ] + Terminal Value / (1+WACC)ⁿ

Terminal Value = FCFₙ × (1+g) / (WACC − g)
✓ Best for:
Stable FCF businesses, utilities, consumer staples, industrial companies with predictable capex
✗ Weakest for:
Early-stage companies, highly cyclical businesses, financial companies (use DDM instead)
Key insight: "Garbage in, garbage out." A DCF is only as good as your assumptions. The terminal value often represents 60–80% of total value — which is why terminal growth rate is the most dangerous input. Graham himself was skeptical of DCF for this reason.
2. Comparable company analysis (trading comps) ↓

Value the company relative to similar public companies. The market tells you what it's willing to pay for comparable businesses. Value investors use this to find where a company is cheap relative to peers — not to justify overpaying.

Key multiples
EV/EBITDA — enterprise value to operating earnings before D&A
P/E — price to earnings (most common, most abused)
EV/Revenue — useful for early-stage or low-margin businesses
P/FCF — price to free cash flow (most honest)
P/Book — price to book value (Graham's favorite)
SectorTypical EV/EBITDATypical P/EWhat drives premium
Industrials8–12x14–20xBacklog visibility, margins
Consumer staples12–18x18–26xBrand strength, pricing power
Mining5–8x10–16xCommodity cycle, reserves

Technology20–40x30–60xGrowth expectations (not value)
3. Asset-based valuation (Net Asset Value) ↓

The most fundamental Graham approach — what would this business be worth if liquidated today? For financial balance sheet investors, this is the anchor. Growth is upside, not the base case.

Asset-based valuation hierarchy
1. Liquidation Value = Cash + (0.85×Receivables) + (0.5×Inventory) − All Liabilities
2. NCAV = Current Assets − Total Liabilities (Graham's net-net)
3. Book Value = Total Assets − Total Liabilities
4. Adjusted Book = Book + Hidden asset adjustments (real estate, IP, etc.)
Hidden assets to look for: Owned real estate carried at historical cost, undervalued subsidiaries, accumulated NOLs (tax assets), brand value not on balance sheet, underfunded/overfunded pension.
4. Dividend Discount Model (DDM) ↓

Best for mature dividend-paying companies. Value = present value of all future dividends. Most appropriate for banks, utilities, REITs, and insurance companies where free cash flow is hard to define.

Gordon Growth Model (constant growth DDM)
Intrinsic Value = D₁ / (r − g)

D₁ = Next year's dividend, r = Required return, g = Constant growth rate
5. Earnings Power Value (EPV) — Graham & Dodd method ↓

Bruce Greenwald's approach. Separate what the current business earns (EPV) from what future growth might add. True value investing starts with EPV — growth is bonus.

Earnings Power Value
EPV = Adjusted EBIT × (1 − tax rate) / WACC

Where "Adjusted EBIT" normalizes for cycle, one-offs, and accounting distortions.
Growth value = Strategic value − EPV (only counted if competitive advantage exists)
The key insight: If EPV > Asset Value, the company earns above its cost of capital — it has a real moat. If EPV < Asset Value, competition will erode returns. EPV = Asset Value is the competitive equilibrium.

Section IV

Economic moats.

Buffett's term for durable competitive advantage. A moat is what allows a business to earn above its cost of capital for years or decades without competition eroding those returns. Identifying moats is the most important skill in quality-oriented value investing.

🏰
Network effects
The product becomes more valuable as more people use it. The strongest and rarest moat type — almost impossible to dislodge once established. Winner-takes-most dynamics.
Examples: Visa, Mastercard (payment networks), MSCI (index data), Bloomberg Terminal
💊
Switching costs
Customers are locked in because the cost of switching — financial, operational, psychological — is too high. Enterprise software, financial systems, specialized industrial equipment.
Examples: Veeva Systems, Costar, industrial ERP providers, core banking software
💰
Cost advantages
Structural cost advantage — access to cheaper resources, proprietary processes, economies of scale, or better location. Can serve customers at a price competitors can't match profitably.
Examples: Nucor (mini-mills), GEICO (direct distribution), Walmart (supply chain scale)
🧠
Intangible assets
Brands, patents, licenses, regulatory approvals. The brand allows pricing above commodity; patents provide a legal monopoly; licenses (like bank charters) limit competitors by law.
Examples: Coca-Cola (brand), Novo Nordisk (patents), regulated utilities (licenses)
🏗️
Efficient scale
In markets of limited size, one or a few firms serve the market profitably — but new entrants can't because they'd reduce returns for everyone. Natural geographic monopolies.
Examples: Regional airports, waste management in small markets, local pipelines
⚠️
No moat — value traps
Cheap stocks with no competitive advantage. Eventually competition erodes earnings to cost of capital. Statistically cheap, fundamentally deteriorating. Graham-style screens catch some; moat analysis filters them out.
Avoid: Commodity manufacturers, undifferentiated retailers, businesses where "good enough" competition exists
Moat durability — how to assess
ROIC — the moat scorecard↓

Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) is the single best indicator of moat strength. A business earning 25% ROIC year after year in a competitive industry has a moat — there's no other explanation.

ROIC formula
ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital
NOPAT = EBIT × (1 − tax rate)
Invested Capital = Total Assets − Excess Cash − Non-interest-bearing current liabilities
ROIC levelInterpretation
< 8%Below cost of capital — value destroyer
8–12%Mediocre — competitive equilibrium
12–20%Good business with some advantage
> 20%Strong moat — exceptional business
The 5 questions to identify a moat↓
1. Has ROIC exceeded cost of capital for 10+ years consistently?
2. What stops a well-funded competitor from copying this business tomorrow?
3. Can the company raise prices without losing customers?
4. Have gross margins been stable or expanding over time?
5. Does management reinvest capital at high returns (not just distribute it)?
Section V

Financial statement analysis.

Value investors are forensic accountants. You're looking for the truth behind the numbers — what management reports vs. what the business actually does.

The three statements — how they connect
Income statement
Earnings quality
What to look for:
— Revenue growth trend (organic vs acquired)
— Gross margin stability or expansion
— SG&A as % of revenue (operational leverage)
— R&D investment vs competitors
— Non-recurring items — are they really non-recurring?
— EPS vs FCF per share divergence (accounting manipulation signal)

Red flags: Revenue growth without margin expansion, frequent "one-time" charges, channel stuffing, aggressive revenue recognition
Balance sheet
Asset quality & leverage
What to look for:
— Cash and equivalents (fortress or barely solvent?)
— Receivables days outstanding trend (rising = warning)
— Inventory days (rising = can't sell; falling = efficiency)
— Goodwill as % of assets (acquisition history)
— Debt maturity schedule (refinancing risk)
— Off-balance-sheet liabilities (leases, pension)

Red flags: Goodwill > 50% of assets, rising DSO, debt covenants near limits, large unfunded pensions
Cash flow statement
The truth teller
What to look for:
— Operating CF vs Net Income (should track closely)
— Capex intensity (maintenance vs growth capex)
— Free cash flow conversion (FCF/Net Income)
— Working capital changes (hidden cash drain?)
— Financing activities (debt issuance, buybacks, dividends)

Green flag: FCF > Net Income consistently = conservative accounting, real cash generation Red flag: Net Income >> FCF for multiple years = earnings manipulation

Forensic accounting — what management hides
Channel stuffing & revenue manipulation↓

Management ships product to distributors (recognizes revenue) knowing much will be returned. Watch: Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) rising faster than revenue growth. If DSO expands significantly, receivables are being counted as revenue that won't be collected.

DSO check
DSO = (Accounts Receivable / Revenue) × 365
Rising DSO → potential revenue manipulation
Adjusted EBITDA abuse↓

Companies add back stock comp, restructuring, "one-time" items to create "Adjusted EBITDA." If adjusted EBITDA >> GAAP EBITDA every year, the "adjustments" are actually recurring costs. Stock-based comp is real dilution — never ignore it.

Goodwill impairment as a warning signal↓

When a company overpays for acquisitions, it creates goodwill. Goodwill impairment = management admitting they overpaid. Watch: acquisitive companies with goodwill > 40% of total assets. They are buying growth instead of creating it — and overpaying for it.

Maintenance vs growth capex↓

Companies don't break out maintenance capex. Buffett's rule: "Owner earnings = Net Income + D&A − Maintenance CapEx." A capital-intensive business with high depreciation may have near-zero true FCF. Always ask: what would FCF be if they stopped growing?

Buffett's owner earnings
Owner Earnings = Net Income + D&A − Maintenance CapEx ± Working Capital changes
Section VI

The macro lens.

Value investors don't forecast the economy — but they need to understand the environment. Cycle positioning, interest rate impact on valuation, and industry-specific macro drivers are all essential.

Interest rates & valuation
The denominator effect
When rates rise, the discount rate rises, and intrinsic values fall — especially for long-duration assets (high-growth stocks, infrastructure). Value stocks (short duration — near-term cash flows) are relatively protected. Graham's bond yield screen (Screen #1) captures this directly: as bond yields rise, the P/E you should pay falls.

Rule of thumb: Every 100bps rise in rates → 10-year P/E multiple compression of 1–2 turns on average.
Business cycles & sector rotation
When to own what
Early cycle — Financials, consumer discretionary, industrials recover first
Mid cycle — Technology, materials, capital goods. Peak earnings growth.
Late cycle — Energy, utilities, healthcare outperform. Value holds better.
Recession — Consumer staples, utilities, healthcare. Cash is king.
Value investors don't time cycles — but cycle awareness prevents buying cyclical industrials at peak earnings (which look cheap on P/E but aren't on normalized earnings).
The normalized earnings concept: Never value a cyclical company on peak or trough earnings. Use 7-10 year average earnings (Graham's preferred period). A steel company earning $10/share at peak may earn $2 in a recession. The "cheap" 8x P/E at peak is actually 40x normalized.
Core Framework — Bruce Greenwald

The three strata of corporate value.

The intellectual heart of the value investing curriculum. Value a business in three sequential layers — from most reliable to least. Each layer builds on the one before. Never pay for a higher stratum unless the lower one is confirmed.

The core principle: Information reliability decreases as you move up the strata. Asset value is grounded in today's balance sheet — it's real. Earnings power is an estimate of normalized cash flows — reasonably reliable. Growth value is a forecast of the future — the least certain and most speculative. A true value investor anchors to the bottom, treats the middle as the primary lens, and treats growth as upside — never as the thesis.
1
Most reliable · Balance sheet anchor
Asset value — reproduction cost
The most reliable data is what the company owns right now. You calculate the Reproduction Cost of Assets — how much money would it cost a well-financed competitor to rebuild this exact business from scratch today? This is your floor. If the market cap is below reproduction cost, you're buying assets for less than it would cost to create them.
Reproduction cost of assets
Start with book value → adjust tangible assets to replacement cost
Add: brand value, customer relationships, regulatory licenses
Subtract: obsolete equipment, impaired goodwill
= True reproduction cost a competitor would face
When to rely on it most
Asset-heavy businesses: utilities, real estate, pipelines, regulated infrastructure, mining. The physical assets ARE the business.
Graham's application
His net-net screen (buy at <2/3 NCAV) is pure stratum 1 investing — buying assets at a discount before even considering earning power.
2
Primary lens · Greenwald's contribution
Earnings power value (EPV)
Developed by Bruce Greenwald. EPV assumes the company will never grow — it will simply sustain its current level of normalized earnings forever. You adjust earnings for cycle, one-offs, and accounting distortions, then capitalize at the cost of capital. This is what the business is worth with zero growth priced in.
Earnings Power Value formula
EPV = Adjusted EBIT × (1 − tax rate) / WACC

Step 1: Start with reported EBIT
Step 2: Adjust for cyclicality (use 7–10 year average margins)
Step 3: Add back excess D&A over maintenance capex
Step 4: Remove truly non-recurring items (be skeptical)
Step 5: Capitalize normalized after-tax earnings at WACC
The critical signal — EPV vs Asset Value:
• EPV >> Asset Value → company earns well above cost of capital → competitive moat confirmed
• EPV ≈ Asset Value → competitive equilibrium, no moat, average returns
• EPV < Asset Value → returns below cost of capital → avoid or wait for restructuring
Best applied to
Mature, stable businesses with consistent earnings history. Regulated utilities, consumer staples, industrials with long operating records.
The Socratic case
A regulated Western European utility with inflation-linked monopoly returns → EPV is the dominant lens. Assets anchor the floor; EPV captures the perpetual, predictable cash stream. Growth is irrelevant.
3
Least reliable · Only if moat confirmed
Growth value — future franchise
The most speculative tier. Value investors only pay for growth if — and only if — the company possesses a definitive structural barrier to entry that prevents competitors from stealing those future profits. Without a moat, growth creates no value for shareholders because competition will erode returns back to the cost of capital before the growth materializes.
Growth value formula
Growth Value = Strategic Value − EPV

Strategic Value = EPV assuming growth at above-WACC returns
Growth Value is only positive if ROIC on new investment > WACC
Without moat: Growth Value = $0 (competition eliminates the surplus)
The most common mistake in investing: Paying for growth value in businesses without a moat. A growing commodity business, a growing retailer with no pricing power, a growing manufacturer with no switching costs — the growth creates no lasting value. Competitors enter, returns normalize, and the premium you paid evaporates.

Warren Buffett's framework

Buffett's four tenets.

Buffett's investment framework organized into four categories. Not a checklist to run mechanically — a philosophy to internalize. Every great Berkshire investment passes all four.

Tenet 1
Business tenets
Simple and understandable
If you can't explain how the business makes money in two sentences, you don't understand it. Buffett's circle of competence principle — complexity is a red flag, not a feature.
Consistent operating history
Stable, predictable operating earnings over many years. Buffett avoids turnarounds — "a good jockey does best on a good horse." 10+ years of consistent results is the minimum signal.
Favorable long-term prospects
The business must be in an industry that will still matter in 20 years. Buffett refused to invest in newspapers once digital disruption became clear — even though they were cheap. Industry tailwinds matter.
Tenet 2
Management tenets
Candid and transparent
Managers who acknowledge mistakes, explain failures honestly, and communicate bad news as readily as good news. Red flag: annual reports that only celebrate wins. Buffett reads competitor annual reports to understand what honest disclosure looks like.
Leaders, not followers
Management that resists institutional imperative — the tendency to copy competitors, make unnecessary acquisitions, and chase trends. The best managers do what's right for the business, not what Wall Street rewards in the short term.
Rational capital allocation
The most important management skill. Retained earnings should be deployed where they earn the highest return — buybacks when stock is cheap, dividends when reinvestment opportunities are scarce, acquisitions only at rational prices.
Tenet 3
Financial tenets
High return on equity (owner earnings)
Buffett uses a modified ROE based on owner earnings rather than reported net income. Must be consistently above 15% without excessive leverage.
Owner earnings
Owner Earnings = Net Income + D&A − Maintenance CapEx ± Working Capital Δ
High and stable profit margins
Gross margins and operating margins should be stable or expanding over time. Declining margins signal competitive erosion — someone is taking pricing power away from the business. Buffett wants managers who control costs obsessively.
For every dollar retained, at least one dollar of market value created
Buffett's famous test: over a rolling 5-year period, has $1 of retained earnings created at least $1 of market value? If not, management is destroying capital. Dividends would have served shareholders better.
Tenet 4
Market tenets
Use conservative estimates + riskless rate
Buffett discounts future earnings at the long-term US Treasury rate — not an inflated WACC — because he only buys businesses he's certain about. Certainty replaces the risk premium. Then he uses conservative, not optimistic, earnings estimates.
Buy at attractive prices when investors turn away
In keeping with the Mr. Market view — the best prices appear when the investment is unpopular, misunderstood, or temporarily impaired. Buffett bought heavily during 1973–74, 2008–09, and every major market dislocation. Fear creates opportunity.
Determine intrinsic value, then require margin of safety
Calculate conservatively, then demand a further discount. The margin of safety is not just theoretical protection — it's the source of above-average returns. Buying at 60 cents on the dollar means you profit on the way to fair value even if your growth thesis is wrong.
How to use Buffett's tenets in practice: Go through each tenet before writing your investment thesis. If a company fails on management candor (Tenet 2) or has declining margins (Tenet 3), stop — no valuation can save a bad business run by bad managers. Buffett has said the biggest mistakes of his career were buying mediocre businesses at cheap prices, not expensive businesses at rich prices.
Section — Quality of Earnings Deep Dive

Accounting skepticism.

University value investing courses spend weeks on this. Audited financial statements are the starting point — not the answer. Your job is to find what the numbers are hiding.

Working capital — the hidden cash drain
Red flag: inventory piling up
Inventory / sales ratio rising
When inventory grows faster than revenue, the company is producing more than it's selling. Classic warning sign of:
— Future write-downs (inventory marked down or scrapped)
— Margin compression (markdowns needed to clear stock)
— Working capital cash drain (cash tied up in unsold goods)
— In macro context: rising inventory was one of the Q4 2024 GDP red flags
Inventory days (DIO)
DIO = (Inventory / COGS) × 365 — Rising DIO = warning
Red flag: receivables expanding
Days Sales Outstanding rising
Revenue is recognized when goods are shipped — but if customers aren't paying, it's not real cash. DSO rising faster than revenue growth = classic channel stuffing or aggressive recognition.

Also check: accounts receivable as % of revenue trend. If receivables grow 30% while revenue grows 10% — something is wrong.
Days Sales Outstanding
DSO = (Accounts Receivable / Revenue) × 365
Compare to prior years and industry peers
Capital expenditure — the most manipulated line item
Maintenance capex vs growth capex — why it matters↓

Companies report total capex — they never break out how much is just to keep existing assets running (maintenance) vs how much is building new capacity (growth). A company can make its free cash flow look artificially high by underspending on maintenance capex, letting assets deteriorate. This is particularly common when management is being acquired or when short-term incentives dominate.

Buffett's owner earnings (true FCF)
Owner Earnings = Net Income + D&A − Maintenance CapEx

Maintenance CapEx ≈ D&A for stable businesses
Growth CapEx = Total CapEx − Maintenance CapEx
How to estimate maintenance capex
1. Ask: what would FCF be if the company stopped growing?
2. Compare D&A to capex — for stable businesses they should track closely
3. Check capex vs competitors on a per-unit or per-asset basis
4. Read MD&A disclosures — management sometimes hints at the split
Classic trap: A capital-intensive industrial company with $200M D&A and $50M reported "FCF" — but $180M of that capex is maintenance. True owner earnings are actually negative. The reported FCF is an illusion.
Off-balance sheet liabilities — hidden claims on equity↓

Three major categories of liabilities that don't always appear clearly on the balance sheet but absolutely reduce the value of your equity claim:

Operating leases
Post-IFRS 16 / ASC 842, most leases are on-balance sheet. But check: are there short-term leases excluded? Lease obligations represent real, fixed cash outflows. For retailers, restaurants, and airlines — lease liabilities can dwarf reported debt.
Pension deficits
Defined benefit pension plans create a liability when the plan's assets are less than its obligations. This underfunding is real debt — it must eventually be funded from operating cash flows. Check note disclosures for pension funded status and discount rate assumptions (management can manipulate the discount rate to reduce reported liability).
Contingent liabilities & legal
Lawsuits, environmental remediation, product liability — disclosed in footnotes, often vague. Read the legal proceedings section of every 10-K carefully. Companies systematically understate these because "probable and estimable" is a high bar for recognition. Ask: what's the worst-case scenario?

Section — The Capstone Skill

The stock pitch.

The capstone of any university value investing curriculum. You find an unloved, obscure, or misunderstood stock — and you pitch it to a panel of fund managers. This is exactly what Contrapposto Research should produce.

The model: Students are required to find a stock no one else is talking about, perform primary research (talk to competitors, suppliers, customers — not just read sell-side reports), calculate intrinsic value using the three strata framework, and present a buy or sell recommendation with a price target. The panel questions are Socratic and adversarial. You must know the bear case better than the bulls.
The 5-part stock pitch structure
1
Business overview — what does it actually do?
Two sentences max. If you can't do it in two, you don't understand it (Buffett Tenet 1). Name the business model, who pays them, why customers can't easily leave.
2
Why is it misunderstood? — your variant perception
This is the most important slide. Why does the market have it wrong? Temporary impairment? Accounting complexity? Spin-off overhang? Sector sentiment? Without a clear answer, there's no thesis — just hope.
3
Valuation — three strata, football field
Asset value (floor), EPV (primary), growth value (upside if moat confirmed). Show the football field — range of values across methods. State your price target and margin of safety. Be specific.
4
Catalysts — what will close the gap?
Value alone doesn't make money — you need a reason for the gap to close. Earnings inflection, management change, spin-off, activist investor, regulatory resolution, debt paydown. Without a catalyst, you may be right but early — which in finance feels the same as wrong.
5
Risks & bear case — know this better than anyone
State the three most compelling reasons the investment fails. What would make you wrong? What would cause you to sell? A panel will always go here — if you haven't thought it through, you lose credibility on everything else.
Primary research — what separates great pitches
Talk to customers
Are they happy? Would they switch if a competitor offered 10% cheaper? How deeply embedded is the product? This is the moat test in real life — not in a spreadsheet.
Talk to competitors
What do they think of the target company? Where do they compete directly? Where do they avoid competing? Competitor fear is one of the best moat signals available.
Read primary sources
10-K, 10-Q, proxy statements, earnings transcripts — every word, including footnotes. The best information is in what management tries to make boring. If it's hidden, it matters.
Socratic challenge — practice your thinking
Case study
A major regulated electric utility in Western Europe
The company owns transmission lines. It has a government-guaranteed monopoly in its region. Its allowed returns are set by the regulator and tied directly to inflation. It has operated consistently for 40 years.
The question
Which of the three strata do you rely on most — Asset Value, Earnings Power Value, or Growth Value? Why?
The answer — EPV (Stratum 2)
Asset Value anchors the floor — transmission infrastructure is expensive to reproduce and you'd use it to confirm you're not overpaying for the physical assets. But EPV is your primary lens because the government-guaranteed, inflation-linked monopoly earnings are extraordinarily predictable — more reliable than almost any other cash flow stream in finance. You capitalize normalized regulated earnings at a rate reflecting near-zero competition risk.

Growth Value is largely irrelevant — regulated utilities don't grow organically. Any growth comes from new regulated assets (which earn regulated, not excess, returns). You would not pay for growth here. The entire investment thesis is: stable, inflation-protected, monopoly cash flows at a rational multiple. EPV is the answer.
Section VII — Greenblatt's domain

Special situations.

Where M&A meets value investing. Joel Greenblatt's "Stock Market Genius" is the bible here. Special situations create temporary mispricings because institutional investors are forced sellers or can't own the security.

🏭 Spinoffs
When a parent company spins off a subsidiary, institutional holders of the parent often dump the spinoff — it's too small for their mandates. This creates forced selling pressure, not fundamental value destruction. Spinoffs historically outperform the market by 10%+ in years 1–3 post-separation. Look for: management incentives aligned with spinoff (they're getting options in it), parent motivated to help spinoff succeed, spinoff in a different sector than parent (forced selling by sector-specific funds).
Classic examples: Sears spinoff of Dean Witter / Allstate, Philip Morris spinoff of Kraft, GE spinoffs
🔄 Mergers & merger arbitrage
When an acquisition is announced, the target's stock trades below the deal price — the "arb spread" reflects deal completion risk. If you believe the deal closes, buying the spread is a low-risk, time-bounded return. Value investors like Graham used this as a cash alternative. Key analysis: antitrust risk, financing risk, regulatory approval timeline, break-up fee size.
Spread = (Deal price − Current price) / Current price. Annualized if deal closes in 3 months, multiply spread × 4.
📋 Liquidations
Companies being wound down create known future cash flows. If liquidation value > current market price, you're getting paid to wait. Most investors ignore these because there's no exciting story. Graham's net-net approach is essentially a partial-liquidation framework — you're buying assets at a discount and waiting for value to be realized.
Screen for: announced liquidation + market price < estimated liquidation value. Rare but high-conviction when found.
🔃 Recapitalizations & stubs
When a company does a leveraged recap, it pays out a large special dividend funded by debt. The residual "stub" equity often trades cheaply because it's complex and leveraged. Sophisticated analysis of the stub's cash flows relative to its price can reveal significant value.
Greenblatt's canonical example: Marriott 1992 split into Marriott International + Host Marriott. The stub (Host) was deeply mispriced at spin.
📉 Distressed debt & bankruptcies
Companies in or near bankruptcy have complex capital structures. Bonds trade at cents on the dollar. The "fulcrum security" — the class of debt that will be converted to equity in reorganization — is often the best risk-reward. Requires deep credit analysis, legal knowledge, and patience. Klarman and Marks built careers here.
Key concept: identify where in the capital structure value "breaks" — that's your entry point. Above that = full recovery. Below = wipeout.
Section VIII

Mental models.

What separates great investors isn't better spreadsheets — it's better thinking. These are the mental models that define the value investing tradition.

Second-level thinking (Howard Marks)↓

First-level: "This is a great company, I should buy it." Second-level: "This is a great company, but everyone knows it's great, so the price already reflects that." You need to be right about the business AND be right about what the market is missing.

"To outperform the average investor, you have to think differently from the consensus AND be right." — Howard Marks
Inversion (Charlie Munger)↓

Don't just think about how to succeed — think about how you could fail. "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." Before buying, list every reason the investment could fail. If you can't explain the bear case better than the bulls, you don't understand it well enough.

Variant perception↓

To make money in markets, your view must differ from consensus AND you must be right. Ask: "What do I know or believe that the market doesn't?" If your thesis is the same as the sell-side consensus, there's no edge. The best investments often look obviously wrong to most people.

Mr. Market allegory (Graham)↓

Imagine owning a business with a partner named Mr. Market. Every day he offers to buy your share or sell you his at a stated price. Some days he's ecstatic and prices too high. Some days he's depressed and prices too low. You are free to ignore him. Your job is to take advantage of him — never let his emotions influence your assessment of value.

Circle of competence↓

The most important thing in investing is knowing what you know and what you don't. Define your circle honestly. Then only invest inside it. Expand the circle slowly through deliberate study. Buffett passed on Amazon, Google, and Bitcoin — not because he was wrong about them, but because he knew he couldn't value them with confidence.

Reflexivity (George Soros)↓

Markets don't just reflect reality — they shape it. Rising stock prices improve a company's ability to raise capital and make acquisitions, which justifies higher prices. Falling prices do the reverse. Value investors must understand when reflexivity is working against them in distressed situations — falling prices can become self-fulfilling.

Patience as a competitive advantage↓

Most institutional investors are measured quarterly. This creates structural opportunity for patient capital. Buffett's famous "weighted-bat" concept: in investing, unlike baseball, you don't have to swing at every pitch. Wait for your pitch — then swing hard. Inactivity is often the right answer.

"The stock market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient." — Buffett
The Kelly criterion — position sizing↓

How much to bet when you have edge. The Kelly formula: bet size = (edge/odds). Concentration is required for outperformance — but it requires high-conviction ideas. Munger: "Diversification is for people who don't know what they're doing." Buffett rarely holds more than 10 significant positions.

Kelly formula
f* = (bp − q) / b
b = odds, p = probability of win, q = probability of loss (1−p)
